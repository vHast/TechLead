Overview

A system administrator, or sysadmin, or admin is a person who is responsible for the upkeep, configuration, and reliable operation of computer systems, especially multi-user computers, such as servers.

I never took any particular interest about this position, but since I started using Linux and my enthusiasm for the Free Software ethics started to kick in, I checked if there were any Linux certificates that I could get my hands on.

There were a few, CompTIA+, the organization that sets them up, have many certificate for every flavor of system administrator that you'd like to become.

The exams are not that expensive, depending on your country it may cost more or less, in Estonia, where I am located right now, costs around 120$, which is extremely cheap for what it offers.

There are several job offers where they're not asking for the formal college grade, but the certificate that CompTIA+ grants.

Before going any further, investigate what kind of system administrator you're interested to become and once you do that, come back.

And it will be useful to check your local job market for the certificates people are asking for.

The Path

Once you've chosen which certificate you're interested in, several websites will offer a plan to train you to pass the exam (these exams need to be passed every 3 years or so, depending on the certificate).

My suggestion is to find a buddy that could lend you a .pdf with the contents of the course to see if you're really interested in and stick with it for a month or two to see if it really clicks on you.

Once you've made your decision, depending on your level of expertise you could choose between paying for an interactive course with "labs" (which is just a fancy word for an environment setup for whichever activity you're supposed to do, similar to freeCodeCamp exercises), which is alright if you're new into the tech industry.

Or you could go self-taught, studying from the book and creating the environment yourself (for example, I am interested in Linux+, I should install Rocky Linux to get hands on server experience and practice there) and checking for resources.

CompTIA+ books offer guidelines on how to study and how to setup everything properly, and I personalyl think that if you learn to do that in your own it's way more valuable.

It's going to take a while to study and reach the exam, all I can say is that you should invest time in it and in the end the effort will be worth it, as I write this I can't wait to get to keep studying on my Linux+ certificate that I'm working towards!
